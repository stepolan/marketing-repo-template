The old rule was 30-60 grams of carbohydrate per hour.  The research has moved.  For efforts over three hours, trained athletes can absorb and oxidize 90-120 grams per hour with the right carbohydrate ratios.  Most ultra-runners are still fueling to the old number and wondering why they bonk at mile 60.

The gap between what the literature now supports and what most athletes actually do is not small.  It is, in many cases, the difference between finishing strong and walking the last ten miles.

## What Changed

For decades, 60 grams per hour was the ceiling.  The limit was not in the muscles.  It was in the gut.  The small intestine has a transport capacity for glucose that maxes out around 60 grams per hour.

Then researchers figured out that if you combine glucose and fructose in roughly a 2:1 ratio, you use two different transporters.  That doubles the absorption rate.

## The Practical Implication

For any effort over three hours, the athlete who fuels at 90+ grams per hour consistently outperforms the athlete fueling at 60.  The effect is not subtle.  It shows up in heart rate, perceived effort, and pace in the final third of the race.

This requires training the gut.  You cannot go from 45 grams per hour to 90 on race day.  The absorption capacity is trainable, and it takes about four to six weeks of progressive fueling during long workouts.
